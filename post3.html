Systems & operations
Post 3 · Week 2 · Monday April 6

In hospice, scale isn't about the bottom line. It's about how far your reach extends into communities where someone is suffering alone because someone told them it was too early.

There is a patient right now in a community your organization doesn't yet serve who is managing symptoms alone that don't have to be unmanageable.

Someone told them it was too early for hospice. Or they heard the word hospice and understood it to mean that death was imminent — days, not a journey. Or no one told them anything at all, because no one from your organization had yet built the relationships in that community that would have made the referral possible.

That is what scale means in hospice. Not market share. Reach.

Early in my career I had the privilege of being part of Crossroads Hospice as it grew — rapidly, and with deliberate intention — into new communities. What made that growth remarkable wasn't the speed. It was that patient focus remained at the helm of every decision throughout. The systems built to support scale were built in service of the mission, not in spite of it.

That experience shaped how I approach operations work today.

The shift I help organizations make — from a business held together by a few dedicated people to one built on systems that hold regardless of who is in the room — is not about efficiency for its own sake. It's about creating the operational foundation that makes it possible to say yes to the next community, the next referral source, the next family that hasn't found you yet.

Because here is what I know from that work:

The family that equates hospice with giving up isn't wrong to be afraid. They've been failed by a lack of education somewhere in their journey. And the organization that can reach them — that has the systems to respond consistently, to staff appropriately, to train its team to meet that fear with patience and clarity — that organization changes what hospice means to that family.

There is also the patient who could have graduated from hospice care. Who stabilized. Who had more time than the diagnosis suggested. Who deserved a supported journey rather than a panicked one — and got it, because your organization was structured well enough to be there.

A business that runs on heroics and institutional memory held in one person's head cannot scale into those communities. It is too fragile to carry the weight of that mission.

Building the systems isn't the opposite of the mission. It is what makes the mission sustainable.

Reflection
Is there a community within reach of your organization right now where someone is suffering through manageable symptoms alone — and the only thing standing between them and your care is that you haven't built the infrastructure to get there yet?
